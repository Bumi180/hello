ANCIENT WISDOM

Traditional Chinese Medicine

Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) works by restoring balance in the body, focusing on overall wellness rather than just treating symptoms. At its core, TCM believes good health depends on the balance of Qi (vital energy), Yin-Yang, and the Five Elements.

  • Hot vs Cold Energy: If your body has too much yin or yang, it can drain your qi and create health problems.
  • Prevention Over Cure: Chinese medicine is restorative and promotes balance to break cycles of stress.
  • Common TCM Tea Principles: Ginger for warming, Green Tea for cooling, Chrysanthemum for clearing heat.

Wabi-Sabi

Wabi-sabi is a Japanese philosophy centered on the acceptance of transience and appreciation of beauty in imperfection. Developed out of the aesthetic philosophy of cha-no-yu (the tea ceremony) in fifteenth-century Japan.

Your tea doesn't need to be perfect. The chipped cup, the slightly burnt kettle, the herbs that didn't measure exactly right - these "imperfections" add character and authenticity to your practice.

The ceremony is an elegant and mindful practice that cherishes simplicity, naturalness, and the beauty of imperfection.

Ayurveda

Ayurveda is India's traditional, natural system of medicine that has been practiced for more than 5,000 years. A Sanskrit word, Ayurveda translates as "science of life" or "practices of longevity."

Kapha

TO TEACUP

My Three-Layer Blending Philosophy

When I first started creating my own tea blends, I found myself overwhelmed by endless possibilities. The breakthrough came when I was looking for perfume. Catching a whiff of a complex, layered fragrance, it suddenly clicked. Tea blending follows the same principles as perfume making.

Base Notes: The Foundation

Just like base notes are the foundation of fragrance, the health benefits form the foundation of my tea blends. These are the deeply therapeutic herbs that do the real work. The chamomile for sleep, the ginger for digestion, the tulsi for stress relief.

  • The largest portion of the blend (40-60%)
  • The reason you're drinking the tea
  • The herbs with the most research behind them
  • Often milder in taste but powerful in effect

Middle Notes: The Character

In tea, these are the flavors that make you want to keep sipping—the warming spices, the floral hints, the earthy undertones that give personality to your blend.

  • The character of your tea (20-40%)
  • What makes each blend unique and memorable
  • The bridge between therapeutic and delicious
  • The flavors that linger pleasantly on your palate

Top Notes: The Harmony

These are the accent herbs that tie everything together. The pop of citrus peel, the warming touch of cardamom, the subtle complexity that makes you pause and think, "What is that lovely note?"

  • The finishing touches (10-20%)
  • Often the most fragrant or intense flavors
  • Used sparingly but with big impact
  • The elements that create harmony between base and middle

TEA CRAFT BASICS

Understand Your Base Tools and Methods

True Teas vs Tisanes

True teas come from the Camellia sinensis plant. These include black, green, oolong, and white tea. They all contain caffeine.

Tisanes are herbal teas like chamomile, mint, and rooibos. These are naturally caffeine-free.

Water Temperature

Green tea is best brewed with water between 160 and 180°F. Don't let it boil.

Black tea prefers hotter water, around 200 to 212°F.

Herbal teas do well with water between 205 and 212°F.

Steeping Time

Steep herbal teas for 5 to 10 minutes.

Steep true teas for 2 to 5 minutes.

Longer steeping extracts more flavor and beneficial compounds, but can also increase bitterness in true teas.

Storage

Keep your blends in airtight containers. Glass jars, tins, or paper bags with a seal work well.

Store them in a cool, dry place away from sunlight.

Label your blends with the name and date. This keeps the flavor fresh and helps you remember what's inside.

ESSENTIAL
EQUIPMENT

You don't need fancy gadgets to make beautiful tea. Like wabi-sabi teaches us, less is more. After years of blending, my entire setup fits on a small kitchen counter and costs under $20.

Kettle

Any basic kettle works perfectly. Electric, stovetop, whatever you have. The water temperature matters more than the kettle itself.

Mortar & Pestle

This is my secret weapon. Crushing whole spices like cardamom pods or cinnamon bark releases their essential oils, creating deeper, richer flavors than pre-ground spices ever could.

Mug

That's it. Just a regular mug. Mine has a small chip on the handle from years of use, and it makes every cup taste like home.

My Process

  1. Heat water in the kettle
  2. Crush any whole spices in the mortar & pestle
  3. Add herbs directly to the mug
  4. Pour hot water over everything
  5. Let it steep
  6. Drink with the herbs still in the cup
